A methodology read for 300 AI faculty

A read on where AI fits in UF's stack, and where it sits above the compute.

Thesis01

The compute layer is solved. The curriculum mandate is in motion.

HiPerGator 4.0 went live with 504 NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs in October. The AI² Center coordinates course development across all 16 colleges. More than 300 AI-focused faculty teach roughly 14,000 students per year.

The interesting question now is the one layer up. How do 300 faculty, 7,000 HiPerGator users, and 67 IFAS county extension offices share a methodology for organizing agent context, prompt libraries, and reproducibility artifacts, so that work in one college is legible to work in another.

That question is the subject of a paper Jake Van Clief published with the Interpretable Context Methodology group (ACM TiiS, MIT-licensed repo). ICM proposes a layered filesystem for agent context. Every research group organizes context somehow. ICM is an argument for making that structure explicit, inspectable, and portable between labs.

L0
Identity. Who the agent is, what mission it is on.
L1
Orientation. Institutional context, constraints, vocabulary.
L2
Contracts. Stage gates, inputs, outputs, what "done" looks like.
L3
Reusable factory. Prompts, templates, patterns shared across projects.
L4
Working artifacts. The per-run files. Inputs, outputs, evals, logs.
Framing02

The 60/30/10 view, applied to UF's stack.

Most of what a research group actually builds around an AI workflow is conventional software: file I/O, databases, scheduled jobs, dashboards. In our experience the ratio is roughly:

60%
Layer one
Traditional code and database work. File I/O, schedulers, pipelines, dashboards.
30%
Layer two
Rule-based logic. Deterministic routing, validation, policy gates, schema checks.
10%
Layer three
Genuine LLM work. The part that benefits from HiPerGator and Blackwell.

The research leverage comes from putting each piece on the correct layer. When ICM is used as the backbone, the LLM calls, the deterministic logic, and the data-layer code each live where they belong, and reproducibility becomes a property of the directory tree rather than a separate compliance task.

A prompt-library pattern developed in UF Health Informatics should be readable by a grad student in IFAS without a translation layer.

A faculty working group in Engineering should be able to hand a course template to a working group in Journalism, and the template should still parse. That is what a shared filesystem convention delivers.

Precedent03

Research-grade rigor, peer-level engagement, published output.

The closest analog is a research collaboration with the UK and Ireland Computing Education Research (UKICER) community out of Edinburgh, where Jake completed the MSc in Future Governance. The engagement was academic-peer. Methodology workshopped with faculty, adapted to local constraints, outputs published. The same posture is the right one here.

A smaller Florida precedent exists at Flagler College: a small-cohort faculty workshop applying the same methodology at a teaching-institution scale. Useful if the first touchpoint at UF is a single department rather than the AI² Center directly.

Other contextual proof that informs how we work rather than what we pitch: 1,500+ people trained across enterprise engagements since May 2025 (Pacific Life and Colgate-Palmolive via Correlation One), executive-level cohorts delivered to KPMG UK (Big Four), and a collaboration with the Academy of International Affairs NRW on AI and governance. The Edinburgh and Flagler precedents are the ones we expect to matter in a UF conversation.

The paper04

Interpretable Context Methodology: folder structure as agent architecture.

Submitted to ACM TiiS. MIT-licensed repo with worked examples, a 52-member practitioner community, and a documented U-shaped edit pattern (most contributor activity at L1 orientation and L4 working artifacts, with a stable middle).

  • Venue ACM Transactions on Interactive Intelligent Systems (TiiS). Submitted.
  • Repository github.com/RinDig/Interpretable-Context-Methodology-ICM- (MIT license)
  • Related Microservice Workflow Patterns, a companion paper on distributed agent architectures. Co-authored in the ICM thread.
  • Adjacent work Ethics Engine, a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs. arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 and github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine. Relevant to UF's "Ethics, Data, and Technology" requirement if the conversation goes that direction.
First step05

What a first engagement looks like.

A half-day methodology workshop with the AI² Center or a single college. UF Health Informatics, IFAS, and CISE are the strongest first-fit venues, based on the maturity of their internal agent work and the density of applied AI projects.

Half-day workshop, scoped sprint

  • 01ICM walkthrough with faculty participants, layered by research context and course context.
  • 02Two worked examples against UF-native stacks (HiPerGator job layout, a UF Health-style clinical agent shell).
  • 03Starter repo each participant takes home. MIT license, ready to fork into a lab or a course.
  • 04Short evaluation note after the session, mapped to the AI Blue Sky Task Force recommendations.
  • 05If the methodology fits, the second engagement is a cross-college working session.
